Hiking Equipment List

Basic Clothes

Warm/Dry Weather Basic Clothes

Footwear: A well-fitting pair of hiking boots, keep trying different styles until you find a pair you like. And padded socks, try to find ones that are not meant for cold weather. Look up different lacing techniques to find one that works best for you.
Base layer: A decent T-shirt and shorts, cargo/jean shorts are recommended.
Headwear: Something to block the sun, like a baseball or sun hat. A cowboy hat also works.

Cold/Wet Weather Basic Clothes

Base layer: long underwear, pants, and long-sleeve shirt.
Warmer layer: Some sort of hoodie/jacket to insulate yourself. If you think you might need it, stick it in your pack just in case.
Waterproof layer: A raincoat/windbreaker combo would be best, but just waterproof will do fine. As well as a pair of rain pants to keep your legs protected from the wet. Same as the warmer layer, if you think you might need it, stick it in your pack.
Footwear: A good pair of hiking boots, along with special hiking socks. They have extra padding to prevent blisters, and they keep your feet warm in cold weather.

Basic Gear

Universal/Day Hiking Gear

Backpack: Since you will be traveling along many miles of trails, you will want a light pack, (Light pack, meaning the bag itself is light), because the things you pack will be heavier than you expect after 3+ miles of trail.
Navigation: A GPS, your phone, or a classic map and compass will get you through.
Sun protection: A hat and sunscreen will do.
First Aid: A first aid kit from amazon is good, consult a first aid guide to make sure you are stocked properly.
Nutrition: Have a few snacks of your choice on hand.
Hydration: Have a decent sized water bottle, 32-40oz is standard.

Overnight/Backpacking gear

Illumination: For if you plan to hike at night, you will need a source of light to see the trail, as well as spare batteries.
Insulation: This is where you would put your warmer clothes in your pack for when the temperature drops at night, depending on how cold you expect the night to be, or even some additional clothes like gloves or a scarf.
Fire starter: For emergency fire lighting purposes, cooking and warming up. A flint and steel set will do.
Collapsable shelter To keep the elements out at night, also in case everything goes wrong. There are compact, lightweight shelters available that will fit in your bag

Extra equipment

These pieces of equipment are not make-or-break, but they are nice to have.
Bug spray, watch, emergency blanket, duct tape, gloves, trekking poles for steep ascents/descents, extra socks, whistle, and if you are hiking during hunting season, wear an orange vest.

Bear/Wildlife Country

For when you are in Bear Country, or just generally around wildlife, carry Bear spray or Pepper spray if you can't get Bear spray, and make sure to train yourself to use it effectively. An airhorn can be effective in intimidation/alarm for other nearby hikers.

Refer to the tips and tricks list for more information on wildlife defense.

Ice Equipment

Ice Pick: For climbing sheer icy/slippery slopes.
Crampons: Spiked shoes that are better for traction on icy surfaces.
